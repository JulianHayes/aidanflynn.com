## Prompt 5: Service Architecture and Delivery Framework

Phase C, Run 1 of 5. Core service design.

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Conversation | New conversation within Brand Schema project |
| Web Search | OFF |
| Deep Research | OFF |
| Output | 12-16 page Word document with service architecture, pricing, delivery model |
| Time | 120 minutes |

### Context

```text
You have completed Phase A (market validation) and Phase B (positioning stress test).

Now design the service architecture: what exactly will you sell, at what price, delivered how, and to whom?
```

### Prompt

```text
Build comprehensive service architecture across six steps.

Step 1 (Jobs-to-be-Done): Define 10 jobs-to-be-done in client language, covering AI visibility diagnosis, verbal identity codification, regulatory compliance, competitive benchmarking, conversational persona design, ongoing measurement.

Step 2 (Service Tiers): Design three-tier architecture:
- Tier 1 (Diagnostics): AI Visibility Audit (£20K-£45K), EU AI Act Compliance Check (£12K-£25K), Competitive Citation Snapshot (£5K-£8K). Positioned as urgency generators and upsell mechanisms.
- Tier 2 (Strategic): Verbal Identity Codification (£45K-£75K), Multi-Engine Discoverability Strategy (£30K-£50K), AI Content Governance Framework (£25K-£40K), Conversational Persona Design (£35K-£60K, held to month 12+).
- Tier 3 (Retainers): £1.5K-£5K per month depending on service, creating switching costs and recurring revenue.

Step 3 (Client Journey): Map conversion logic from Tier 1 -> Tier 2 -> Tier 3. What triggers each upgrade?

Step 4 (Pricing Benchmarks): Benchmark pricing against GEO agency and brand consultancy market data.

Step 5 (Delivery Model): Specify founder/AI/partner model. Who delivers what? When do founders step back? When do you hire?

Step 6 (Economics): Capacity analysis, first hire trigger, full three-year client lifetime value calculation (approximately £319K per full-journey client).

For each service, include: one-line description, benefit statement, before-and-after framing, one unique "Why Only Brand Schema" statement.

Flag three decisions to stress-test: should Competitive Citation Snapshot be paid or free? Should Conversational Persona Design be gated to month 12? Are you confident in the 60-70% Tier 2-to-Tier 3 conversion rate assumption?
```

## Prompt 6: Buyer Simulation Exercise

Phase C, Run 2 of 5. Service architecture stress test.

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Conversation | New conversation within Brand Schema project |
| Web Search | OFF |
| Deep Research | OFF |
| Output | 10-14 page Word document with four buyer conversations |
| Time | 90 minutes |

### Context

```text
You have designed service architecture (Prompt 5).

Now simulate buyer conversations to test whether the service architecture actually resonates and whether objections are handled.
```

### Prompt

```text
Generate four detailed buyer simulation conversations. Four buyer personas:

1. Sophie: Head of Brand at financial services firm (£10B+ AUM). 10+ years brand leadership. Knows what good looks like. Will ask hard questions about methodology and proof.

2. James: CMO at automotive company. 5 years in role. Concerned about AI visibility but not yet convinced it's urgent. Will ask about ROI and timeline.

3. Anika: VP Digital at B2B SaaS. 3 years in role. Technically sophisticated. Will ask about integration with existing tools and whether this is actually needed.

4. Richard: Group Marketing Director at retail conglomerate. 15+ years in marketing. Has seen every pitch. Will ask about budget justification and whether existing agencies can add this capability.

For each buyer, generate three phases:

Phase 1 (Enquiry): Buyer inquires. What triggered their inquiry? What do they ask first?

Phase 2 (Pitch): You pitch the Brand Schema Audit and Verbal Identity Codification service. How does the buyer respond? What objections emerge?

Phase 3 (Objection Handling): Buyer raises three specific objections. How do you handle each?

After simulations, synthesise:
- Six gaps in the service architecture exposed by buyer feedback
- Five objection categories with recommended responses
- Three changes to service architecture that would improve buyer credibility

Be realistic. Assume these are intelligent, sceptical buyers with alternatives.
```

## Prompt 7a: Messaging Framework — Layers 1 to 4

Phase C, Run 3 of 5. First half of messaging.

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Conversation | New conversation within Brand Schema project |
| Web Search | OFF |
| Deep Research | OFF |
| Output | 8-10 page Word document |
| Time | 120 minutes |

### Context

```text
You have designed service architecture (Prompt 5) and tested it against buyer reality (Prompt 6).

Now build messaging framework that translates the positioning and service architecture into language that actually moves buyers.
```

### Prompt

```text
Produce Layers 1 through 4 of messaging framework.

Layer 1 (Master Narrative): 250-400 words. Editorial register. Opens with problem, not firm. Explains: why AI visibility matters, why verbal identity is the missing piece, why now, what Brand Schema does differently. One narrative version that all other messaging flows from.

Layer 2 (Elevator Pitches): Three versions: 15-second (conference hallway), 60-second (sales call opening), 3-minute (discovery meeting). Each explains the core idea without jargon.

Layer 3 (Audience-Specific Messaging): Five blocks, each 100-150 words, tailored to:
- CMO: focus on AI visibility risk, brand coherence, competitive advantage
- Head of Digital: focus on technical integration, implementation timeline, resource requirements
- Procurement: focus on value justification, pricing transparency, contract terms
- CFO: focus on ROI calculation, cost per acquisition, lifetime value
- Agency Partner: focus on collaboration model, revenue sharing, capability gap they're filling

Layer 4 (Sector-Specific Angles): Six blocks, each 100-150 words, tailored to:
- Financial services: regulatory compliance, brand trust in AI era, citation frequency in insurance/legal searches
- Automotive: product discovery via voice/AI, dealer network AI visibility, competitive positioning vs Tesla/legacy
- B2B Technology: buyer research behaviour change, thought leadership in AI discovery, sales enablement
- Retail: product recommendations in ChatGPT/Gemini, retail brand visibility, supply chain transparency to AI
- Luxury: brand heritage in AI, exclusivity in voice interface, authentication and counterfeit risk
- FMCG: brand discovery via AI shopping agents, promotional agility, competitor visibility

Style: British English, editorial confidence, no exclamation marks, no superlatives, no "we believe."

Tone: precise, grounded, urgent.
```

## Prompt 7b: Messaging Framework — Layers 5 to 7

Phase C, Run 4 of 5. Second half of messaging.

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Conversation | Same conversation as Prompt 7a |
| Web Search | OFF |
| Deep Research | OFF |
| Output | 10-12 page Word document |
| Time | 120 minutes |

### Context

```text
You have produced Layers 1-4 (narrative, pitches, audience-specific, sector-specific).

Now produce Layers 5-7 (service messaging, evidence bank, language principles).
```

### Prompt

```text
Produce Layers 5 through 7 of messaging framework.

Layer 5 (Service-Level Messaging): For all services in Prompt 5 architecture:
- Tier 1 (Diagnostics): one-line description, benefit statement, before-and-after framing, proof point
- Tier 2 (Strategic): full messaging block with description, benefit, before/after, proof point
- Tier 3 (Retainers): one-line description, one-line benefit, before/after framing only (lighter treatment)

Each service gets a "Why Only Brand Schema" statement that articulates the structural gap in existing provider types.

Layer 6 (Evidence Bank): Organised around four themes, each table with 8-12 data points:
1. Scale of Shift: AI adoption rates, search displacement metrics, brand discovery changes
2. Cost of Inaction: brands losing visibility, competitive citation gaps, marketing efficiency loss
3. Return on Investment: AI-referred visitor conversion rates, lifetime value uplift, cost per qualified lead
4. Differentiation Gap: competitor capability analysis, Brand Schema-only claims, proof points from Prompts 4-6

For each claim: source, verification status (confirmed/training data/unconfirmed), which messaging layer it supports.

Layer 7 (Language Principles):
- Ten banned words and alternatives (no "innovative," "leverage," "seamless," etc.)
- Ten owned phrases (specific to Brand Schema vocabulary)
- Sentence structure preferences (active voice, short sentences, no jargon)
- Jargon handling policy (define or avoid)
- Register guide: same core idea expressed across four channels (thought leadership, proposal, social media, client delivery)

Check consistency with Layer 1 master narrative. Flag any conflicts between 7a and 7b messaging.
```

## Prompt 8: Verbal Identity Guide

Phase C, Run 5 of 5. Brand voice codification.

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Conversation | New conversation within Brand Schema project |
| Web Search | OFF |
| Deep Research | OFF |
| Output | 12-16 page Word document with voice definition, tone rules, vocabulary, AI configuration |
| Time | 120 minutes |

### Context

```text
You have produced complete messaging framework (Prompts 7a + 7b).

Now codify this into a verbal identity guide that ensures consistent Brand Schema voice across all touchpoints and ensures machine readability for AI systems.
```

### Prompt

```text
Produce comprehensive verbal identity guide.

Section 1 (Voice Definition): Four character adjectives defining Brand Schema voice. For each: spectrum from one extreme to another, guidance on when to land where, how to handle difficult moments (angry buyer, technical complexity, competitor FUD).

Section 2 (Tone Modulation): Four contexts (thought leadership, client pitch, objection handling, celebration/social). For each: tone shift guidance, vocabulary adjustments, worked example showing same core message in each context.

Section 3 (Writing Rules):
- Sentence structure preferences: active voice, short sentences (average 12-14 words), one idea per sentence
- Paragraph structure: opening line is always a claim, not setup
- Data usage: always cite, never speculate, flag confidence levels
- Punctuation: no semicolons, em dashes, Oxford commas where possible
- Capitalisation: Brand Schema Audit (always), GEO and AEO (always), AI visibility (lowercase), "the founder's approach" not "The Founders' Approach"
- Acronym handling: spell out once, use acronym thereafter

Section 4 (Vocabulary):
- 25 owned terms Brand Schema uses consistently (e.g., "verbal identity codification," "machine-readable brand," "citation economy," "Share of Model")
- 28 banned words and alternatives (innovative -> distinctive; leverage -> use; seamless -> integrated; etc.)
- 17 jargon entries with plain-English definitions (GEO, AEO, YMYL, llms.txt, Share of Model, etc.)

Section 5 (AI Configuration): Ready-to-paste system prompt for ChatGPT/Claude configured to write in Brand Schema voice. Include: voice attributes, word bank, prohibited words, output format preference.

Section 6 (Worked Examples): Five before-and-after rewrites showing Brand Schema voice applied to different formats (email, proposal section, social post, thought leadership opening, objection response).

Quality Checklist: Five-point checklist for any output claiming to be Brand Schema voice.

Flag decisions: did I add any voice adjectives beyond what emerged from earlier prompts? Did I restrict or expand the banned word list? Confirm these are deliberate choices.
```
